Travel back through time to the Ancient City of Akrágas

Possibly the greatest example of Classic Greek architecture outside of Greece.

The ancient city of Akrágas was a Greek Colony founded on Sicily’s southwestern coast over 2600 years ago. Originally named for a nearby river by colonists from Gela, Crete and Rhodes, it is known today as Agrigento and the former site is referred to as “Valle dei Templi” (Italian), "Vaddi di li Tempri" (Sicilian) or “The Valley of Temples” — a slight misrepresentation considering it’s positioned on a plateau and ridge across two hills.

Akrágas was an important center of Greek civilisation and even referred to by Virgil in his classic work, Aeneid. Ruled by a series of tyrants, Akrágas reached great political power in 480 BCE with the military defeat of Carthage at The Battle of Himera. This led to a period of construction of many of the city’s remarkable temples and a population boom of 200,000 people. The noted philosopher Empedocles wrote of his city’s residents, "...they party as if they will die tomorrow, and build as if they will live for ever." But after 75 years the building came to a stop. Carthage exacted its revenge on Akrágas, sacking and abandoning the great city.

Over the next two centuries Akrágas was repopulated, rebuilt and recaptured — first by the returning Carthaginians, and then by the Romans who renamed it as the Latin equivalent “Agrigentum”. Roman Rule brought flourishing trade to the city’s port but also imposed slavery on the many of the city’s inhabitants. After the Fall of the Roman Empire, the area depopulated, becoming a shadow of its former glory. It was little more than a village when Arab rule spread across Sicily in the 9th Century.

The Temples

The temples of Akrágas may have once numbered as many as ten at the height of the city. Today, seven were known to have existed, a few of which still stand. Each temple is dedicated to a different Greek deity and built of sandstone in the Doric style. The temples are now commonly referred to using the Roman/Latin naming conventions.

As an oligarchy, Akrágas saw a series of tyrants at its helm. The tyrant Theron called for a massive building campaign following the city’s victory over the Cathaginians in the 5th Century BCE and many of the temples were built during the century that followed. These structures have endured and amazed for over two thousand years. In 1787, German author Goethe wrote: “Such a splendid springtime view as today's at sunrise we've never had in our whole lives. On the site of the old and Ancient Citadel… towards the southern end of this green and blossoming terrain does one spot The Temple Of Concordia rising above it, as well as to the East the scant remains of The Temple Of Juno”.

In addition to these temples, Akrágas boasted other architectural wonders throughout its extended history including the city’s port “Porto Empedocle”, the pyramidical “Tomb of Theron” which was likely built to commemorate Roman casualties in the Second Punic War and the grand villas of Hellenistic-Roman style built for the city’s wealthiest residents.

Concordia

One of the best preserved temples in the world, the Temple of Concordia features the often used six-by-thirteen column design. It was repurposed by Christian worshipers as a church dedicated to Saints Peter and Paul in c.600 BCE.

Juno

Dedicated to Hera, the Greek goddess of fertility, it is also also known as the Temple of Hera Lacinia. Though destroyed by the Cathaginians in 406 BCE, many of its columns stand to this day, bearing the burn marks of that attack.

Asclepius

Once the destination of believers seeking relief from sickness, the Temple of Asclepius stood outside the former city walls. Small by comparison, it once was home to a bronze statue of the Greek god, Apollo.

Heracles

Less than ten columns stand from this structure, which was dedicated to the locally-beloved Greek god, Hercules. It is the oldest of the known temples of Akrágas and is thought to have been the first temple built under the rule of Theron.

Dioscuri

An iconic remnant of the modern site, the four column structure representing the Temple of Dioscuri is actually a reconstruction using pieces of temples found throughout the site during more recent archaeological work. It was believed to have been used for rituals including animal sacrifices.

Vulcano

Also called the Temple of Hephaestus, it isn’t known which deity this building was dedicated to. Although only a few remnants remain, it was likely the largest of all the structures in Akrágas.

Zeus

Another monument built in response to the Battle of Himera, it was dedicated to Jupiter/Zeus and would have been the size of a football stadium. One special feature would have been the 38 massive statues that decorated it, each measuring as much as seven metres in height.
