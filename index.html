Travel back through time to the Ancient City of Akrágas

Possibly the greatest example of Classic Greek architecture outside of Greece.

The ancient city of Akrágas was a Greek Colony founded on Sicily’s southwestern coast over 2600 years ago. Originally named for a nearby river by colonists from Gela, Crete and Rhodes, the area is known today as Agrigento and the former site is referred to as “Valle dei Templi” (Italian), "Vaddi di li Tempri" (Sicilian) or “The Valley of Temples” — a slight misrepresentation considering it’s positioned on a plateau and ridge across two hills.

Akrágas was an important center of Greek civilisation and even referred to by Virgil in his classic work, Aeneid. Ruled by a series of tyrants, Akrágas reached great political power in 480 BCE with the military defeat of Carthage at The Battle of Himera. This led to a period of construction of many of the city’s remarkable temples and a population boom of 200,000 people. The noted philosopher Empedocles wrote of his city’s residents, "...they party as if they will die tomorrow, and build as if they will live for ever." But after 75 years the building came to a stop. Carthage exacted its revenge on Akrágas, sacking and abandoning the great city.

Over the next two centuries Akrágas was repopulated, rebuilt and recaptured — first by the returning Carthaginians, and then by the Romans who renamed it as the Latin equivalent “Agrigentum”. Roman Rule brought flourishing trade to the city’s port but also imposed slavery on the many of the city’s inhabitants. After the Fall of the Roman Empire, the area depopulated, becoming a shadow of its former glory. It was little more than a village when Arab rule spread across Sicily in the 9th Century.
